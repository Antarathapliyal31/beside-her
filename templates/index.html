What is Postpartum Depression?
A serious but treatable condition

Postpartum depression is not a character flaw or weakness — it's a complication of childbirth. Not about being a bad mother. About needing support.

For Moms
Recognizing the Signs

Knowing the symptoms is the first step. You deserve support — reaching out is a sign of strength, not weakness.

Emotional Signs

Persistent sadness, excessive crying, feeling worthless or guilty, severe mood swings, loss of interest in things you once loved.

Physical Signs

Fatigue and low energy, changes in appetite, difficulty sleeping even when baby sleeps, body aches and headaches.

Behavioral Signs

Withdrawing from loved ones, difficulty bonding with baby, loss of interest in activities, trouble making decisions.

When to Get Help

Symptoms lasting more than 2 weeks, feelings getting worse, or any thoughts of harming yourself or baby — seek help immediately.

For Partners
How You Can Help

Your support can be the difference between her suffering in silence and her path to recovery.

Listen Without Fixing

Sometimes she just needs to be heard. Resist the urge to solve — being present and validating her feelings can mean everything.

Share the Load

Take initiative with baby care, household tasks, and nighttime feedings. Don't wait to be asked — just step in.

Encourage Professional Help

Gently suggest speaking with a doctor or therapist. Offer to make the appointment together and attend if she wants.

What Not to Say

Avoid "Just snap out of it" or "Other moms handle it fine." These phrases minimize a real medical condition.
